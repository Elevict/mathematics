# Chapter 1.5 Trigonometric Function

## Radian

> [!definition] **Definition** (Radian)
> An angle of 1 **radian** is defined to be the angle at the center of a unit circle which cuts off an arc of length 1, measured counterclockwise. A unit circle has radius 1.

## Sine and Cosine Functions

> [!definition] **Definition** (Sine and Cosine on the Unit Circle)
> The two basic trigonometric functions—the sine and cosine—are defined using a unit circle. An angle of $t$ radians is measured counterclockwise around the circle from the point $(1, 0)$. If $P$ has coordinates $(x, y)$, we define
>
> $$
> \cos(t) = x \text{ and } \sin(t) = y.
> $$

Since the equation of the unit circle is $x^{2} + y^{2} = 1$, writing $\cos^{2} t$ for $(\cos t)^{2}$, we have the identity

$$
\cos ^{2}t \sin ^{2}t = 1.
$$

## Amplitude, Period, and Phase

> [!definition] **Definition** (Amplitude and Period)
> For any periodic function of time, the
> - **Amplitude** is half the distance between the maximum and minimum values (if it exists).
> - **Period** is the smallest time needed for the function to execute one complete cycle.

To describe arbitrary amplitudes and periods of sinusoidal functions, we use functions of the
form

$$
f (t) = A sin(Bt) \ \text{ and } \ g(t) = A cos(Bt),
$$

where $|A|$ is the amplitude and $2\pi/|B|$ is the period.
The graph of a sinusoidal function is shifted horizontally by a distance $|h|$ when $t$ is replaced
by $t − h$ or $t + h$.

Functions of the form $f (t) = A sin(Bt) + C$ and $g(t) = A cos(Bt) + C$ have graphs which
are shifted vertically by $C$ and oscillate about this value.

## The Tangent Function

> [!definition] **Definition** (Tangent Function)
> If $t$ is any number with $\cos(t) \neq 0$, we define the tangent function as follows
>
> $$
> \tan(t) = \frac{\sin(t)}{\cos(t)}
> $$

## Inverse Trigonometric Functions

We define the inverse sine, written “$\arcsin$” or “$\sin^{-1}$,” as the function which gives the preferred solution.

> [!definition] **Definition** (Arcsine)
> For $-1 \leq y \leq 1,$
>
> $$
> \arcsin y = x
> $$
>
> means $\sin(x) = y$ with $-\frac{\pi}{2} < x \leq \frac{\pi}{2}$.

The inverse tangent, written “$\arctan$” or “$\tan^{-1}$,” is the inverse function for the piece of the tangent function having the domain $−\pi/2 < x < \pi/2$. On a calculator, the inverse tangent is usually denoted by $\tan^{-1}$.

> [!definition] **Definition** (Arctan)
> For any $y$,
>
> $$
> \arcsin y = x
> $$
>
> means $\tan(x) = y$ with $-\frac{\pi}{2} < x \leq \frac{\pi}{2}$.
