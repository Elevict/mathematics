# 1.8 Introduction to Linear Transformations

The difference between a matrix equation $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ and the associated vector equation

$$
x_1 \mathbf{a}_1 + \cdots + x_n \mathbf{a}_n = \mathbf{b}
$$ 

is merely a matter of notation. However, a matrix equation $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ can arise in linear algebra (and in applications such as computer graphics and signal processing) in a way that is not directly connected with linear combinations of vectors. This happens when we think of the matrix $A$ as an object that “acts” on a vector $\mathbf{x}$ by multiplication to produce a new vector called $A\mathbf{x}$.

For instance, the equations

$$
\begin{pmatrix}
4 & 3 & 1 & 3 \\
2 & 0 & 5 & 1
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
2 \\
1 \\
1 \\
1
\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
5 \\
8
\end{pmatrix}
$$

and

$$
\begin{pmatrix}
4 & 3 & 1 & 3 \\
2 & 0 & 5 & 1
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
2 \\
4 \\
1 \\
3
\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
0 \\
0
\end{pmatrix}
$$

say that multiplication by $A$ transforms $\mathbf{x}$ into $\mathbf{b}$ and transforms $\mathbf{u}$ into the zero vector.

From this new point of view, solving the equation $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ amounts to finding all vectors $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^4$ that are transformed into the vector $\mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^2$ under the “action” of multiplication by $A$.

The correspondence from $\mathbf{x}$ to $A\mathbf{x}$ is a function from one set of vectors to another. This concept generalizes the common notion of a function as a rule that transforms one real number into another.

## Transformation Functions

A transformation (or function or mapping) $T$ from $\mathbb{R}^n$ to $\mathbb{R}^m$ is a rule that assigns to each vector $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ a vector $T(\mathbf{x}) \in \mathbb{R}^m$. The set $\mathbb{R}^n$ is called the domain of $T$, and $\mathbb{R}^m$ is called the codomain of $T$. The notation $T: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$ indicates that the domain of $T$ is $\mathbb{R}^n$ and the codomain is $\mathbb{R}^m$. For $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n$, the vector $T(\mathbf{x})$ in $\mathbb{R}^m$ is called the image of $\mathbf{x}$ under the action of $T$. The set of all images $T(\mathbf{x})$ is called the range of $T$.

## Example 1

Let

$$
A =
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 3 \\
3 & 5 \\
1 & 7
\end{pmatrix}, \quad
\mathbf{u} =
\begin{pmatrix}
2 \\
1
\end{pmatrix}, \quad
\mathbf{b} =
\begin{pmatrix}
3 \\
2 \\
5
\end{pmatrix}, \quad
\mathbf{c} =
\begin{pmatrix}
3 \\
2 \\
5
\end{pmatrix}
$$

and define a transformation $T: \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^3$ by $T(\mathbf{x}) = A\mathbf{x}$, so that

$$
T(\mathbf{x}) = A\mathbf{x} = 
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 3 \\
3 & 5 \\
1 & 7
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
x_1 \\
x_2
\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
x_1 + 3x_2 \\
3x_1 + 5x_2 \\
x_1 + 7x_2
\end{pmatrix}
$$

### Part (a): Find $T(\mathbf{u})$, the Image of $\mathbf{u}$ under the Transformation $T$.

Compute:

$$
T(\mathbf{u}) = A\mathbf{u} = 
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 3 \\
3 & 5 \\
1 & 7
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
2 \\
1
\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
5 \\
11 \\
9
\end{pmatrix}
$$

### Part (b): Find a Vector $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^2$ whose Image under $T$ is $\mathbf{b}$.

Solve $T(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{b}$, or equivalently $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$. This leads to the system:

$$
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 3 \\
3 & 5 \\
1 & 7
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
x_1 \\
x_2
\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
3 \\
2 \\
5
\end{pmatrix}
$$

Using row reduction, we find:

$$
\mathbf{x} =
\begin{pmatrix}
1.5 \\
0.5
\end{pmatrix}
$$

### Part (c): is there More than One $\mathbf{x}$ whose Image under $T$ is $\mathbf{b}$?

No. The system of equations has a unique solution, as shown by the row reduction.

### Part (d): Determine if $\mathbf{c}$ is in the Range of the Transformation $T$.

To find the answer, solve the system $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{c}$. Row reduction shows the system is inconsistent, so $\mathbf{c}$ is not in the range of $T$.

## Linear Transformations

A transformation $T$ is **linear** if:
1. $T(\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}) = T(\mathbf{u}) + T(\mathbf{v})$ for all $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}$ in the domain of $T$;
2. $T(c\mathbf{u}) = cT(\mathbf{u})$ for all scalars $c$ and all $\mathbf{u}$ in the domain of $T$.

## Example 2

If

$$
A =
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 0 & 0 \\
0 & 1 & 0 \\
0 & 0 & 0
\end{pmatrix}
$$ 

then the transformation $T(\mathbf{x}) = A\mathbf{x}$ projects points in $\mathbb{R}^3$ onto the $x_1x_2$-plane, since

$$
T\left(\begin{pmatrix}
x_1 \\
x_2 \\
x_3
\end{pmatrix}\right)
=
\begin{pmatrix}
x_1 \\
x_2 \\
0
\end{pmatrix}
$$

## Example 3

Let

$$
A =
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 3 \\
0 & 1
\end{pmatrix}
$$ 

The transformation $T: \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2$ defined by $T(\mathbf{x}) = A\mathbf{x}$ is called a **shear transformation**. For instance, $T$ transforms the point

$$
\mathbf{u} =
\begin{pmatrix}
0 \\
2
\end{pmatrix}
$$

into

$$
T(\mathbf{u}) =
\begin{pmatrix}
6 \\
2
\end{pmatrix}
$$
